# Spelt

## What Spelt Is

Spelt (*Triticum spelta*) is an ancient hulled wheat, cultivated for thousands of years before largely being displaced by modern bread wheat. It has seen a revival in artisan baking for its nutty, slightly sweet flavour and perceived digestibility advantages.

Wholemeal spelt retains the bran, germ, and endosperm — giving deeper flavour but demanding more careful handling than white spelt or white wheat.

---

## Gluten: The Critical Difference

Spelt's gluten is fundamentally different from modern wheat:

| Property | Spelt | Modern Bread Wheat |
|---|---|---|
| Gluten strength | Weak | Strong |
| Extensibility | Very high | Moderate |
| Elasticity (spring-back) | Low | High |
| Tolerance to over-handling | Poor | Good |
| Fermentation speed | Faster | Slower |

**Why this matters:** Spelt dough feels strong early in bulk fermentation but becomes noticeably slacker as fermentation progresses, because the weaker gluten network degrades faster. This is the primary reason spelt loaves can collapse — over-fermented or over-handled dough loses structure entirely.

Spelt gluten is dominated by gliadins (extensibility proteins), while modern wheat is dominated by glutenins (elasticity proteins). This is why spelt stretches easily but doesn't spring back.

---

## Hydration Guidance

Wholemeal spelt absorbs water differently from white flour:
- Bran particles absorb water slowly — they need time to fully hydrate
- But the gluten network is weaker, so it can't sustain the structure that high hydration requires

**Target range: 70–75% hydration** for wholemeal spelt.

Starting at 72% is safe for a first bake with a new bag of flour. Once you understand how the dough feels, adjust upward or downward.

Do not chase high hydration — it does not reward you with better structure in spelt the way it can with high-gluten wheat. A tighter, well-fermented dough at 72% will out-perform a slack, over-hydrated one.

---

## Handling Rules

1. **Minimal mixing** — once ingredients are incorporated, stop. Spelt needs roughly half the kneading time of bread wheat (4–6 minutes vs 10–12).
2. **Gentle stretch and folds** — no aggressive slap-and-fold or French fold. Use coil folds or standard stretch-and-folds with a light touch.
3. **Stop bulk fermentation earlier** — aim for 40–50% volume increase, not the 75–100% you'd target with wheat. The dough will continue to ferment in the cold retard.
4. **Handle the shaped loaf as little as possible** — each touch degrades the gluten structure further.
5. **Do not autolyse for longer than 45 minutes** — for wholemeal spelt, 30–45 minutes is ideal to hydrate the bran without over-relaxing the already-extensible gluten.

## Wholemeal vs White Spelt

| | Wholemeal Spelt | White Spelt |
|---|---|---|
| Flavour | Deeper, nuttier, more complex | Milder, cleaner |
| Hydration needed | Slightly higher (bran absorbs) | Slightly lower |
| Gluten behaviour | Bran cuts gluten strands = weaker structure | Stronger than wholemeal |
| Rise | Lower, denser crumb | More open crumb possible |
| Handling | Most delicate | More forgiving |

For open crumb and high oven spring with spelt, a blend (e.g. 70% wholemeal + 30% white spelt, or adding some strong bread wheat) gives more headroom. A 100% wholemeal loaf will be denser — this is expected and correct, not a failure.

---

## Sourdough Fermentation with Spelt

Spelt ferments faster than modern wheat because its starch is more accessible to the wild yeast and bacteria in a sourdough culture. In practice:
- Reduce your bulk fermentation time by ~20–30% compared to an equivalent wheat recipe
- Be more vigilant about dough temperature — a warm kitchen (24°C+) can push a spelt dough past its ideal window quickly
- The sweet flavour of spelt pairs well with a mild sourdough sourness; a lower inoculation (10–15%) and cold retard give the best flavour balance

---

## Nutrition Note

Long-fermented spelt sourdough (12+ hours total) is low in FODMAPs due to the breakdown of fermentable carbohydrates by the sourdough bacteria. This can make it better tolerated by people with IBS or wheat sensitivity — though it is **not** gluten-free and is not suitable for coeliac disease.
