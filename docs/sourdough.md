# Sourdough Principles

## The Starter

A sourdough starter is a live culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria (LAB) maintained in a flour-water mixture. The two organisms work together:
- **Wild yeast** produces CO₂ (rise) and some flavour esters
- **LAB** (primarily *Lactobacillus* species) produce lactic and acetic acids (flavour and preservation)

### Starter Readiness

The ideal time to use a starter is at **peak activity** — when it has doubled (or slightly more) in volume, has a domed top, a bubbly interior, and smells pleasantly tangy/yeasty.

Signs of peak:
- Volume has doubled from fed weight
- Top is domed, not yet collapsed
- Float test: a small spoonful in water floats (adequate gas production)
- Smells active and tangy, not overly acidic or alcoholic

A starter past peak (collapsed, liquidy, sharp/alcoholic smell) will still leaven bread but will give a more sour result and weaker rise. Use a peaked starter for the best balance of rise and flavour.

### Inoculation (Starter Percentage)

The amount of starter added to a dough, expressed as a percentage of total flour weight.

| Inoculation | Effect | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| 5–10% | Slow fermentation, mild flavour, complex tang | Long cold bulk or warm kitchen in summer |
| 15–20% | Balanced — reliable rise, moderate tang | Standard home bake, established starter |
| 20–30% | Faster fermentation, more sour | Young/less active starter, cold kitchen |

**Default recommendation: 15%** — a good balance for an established, peaked starter at typical UK room temperatures (18–22°C).

For spelt specifically, 15% is appropriate. Spelt ferments faster than wheat, so you do not need to compensate with a higher inoculation.

### Rye Starters

Rye flour produces exceptionally active starters. The bran carries a dense, diverse population of wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria, and rye's naturally high enzyme activity converts starches to sugars rapidly — giving the microorganisms more food, faster. This is why a neglected wheat starter often responds well to a single rye feed.

**Hydration:** A rye starter is typically maintained at **100% hydration**, but this does not mean it will look or behave like a liquid wheat starter. Because rye's pentosans (water-binding polysaccharides) absorb several times their weight in water, a 100% rye starter forms a **thick, pasty consistency** — similar to wet beach sand or hummus — rather than a pourable liquid. If a rye starter looks thin, it has probably over-fermented or needs more flour.

**Behaviour differences from wheat:**
- Peaks faster — especially in a warm kitchen, it can double within 3–4 hours
- Smells more assertively sour and earthy, less yeasty-sweet than a wheat starter
- Has a shorter window at peak before collapsing — use it on the way up or at peak, not after

**Using rye to boost a wheat starter:** Adding 10–20% rye flour to a wheat starter feed (by weight, replacing an equivalent amount of the wheat flour) increases activity noticeably without fundamentally changing the starter's character. This is useful in cold kitchens or with a sluggish starter.

**The German tradition (Anstellgut):** Traditional German rye breadmaking maintains a fully mature rye starter — *Anstellgut* — at around 80–100% hydration, used at peak when it reaches a pH of approximately 3.9–4.2. This level of acidity is not merely for flavour; it is essential for controlling rye's amylase enzymes during baking (see [rye.md](flours/rye.md) for detail).

## Autolyse

Autolyse is a rest period for **flour + water only**, before the starter or salt are added.

**Purpose:**
1. Full hydration of the flour (especially important for wholemeal — bran absorbs slowly)
2. Gluten begins to develop passively without mechanical energy
3. Enzymes begin breaking down starches, improving flavour
4. Results in a more extensible, easier-to-shape dough with less mixing required

**Method:**
1. Mix flour and water (hold back ~5% of water to use for dissolving salt later)
2. Mix until no dry flour remains — do not over-mix
3. Cover and rest at room temperature

**Duration:**
- White flour: 20–60 minutes
- Wholemeal/whole grain: 30–60 minutes (bran needs longer to hydrate)
- Wholemeal spelt: **30–45 minutes** (enough to hydrate bran; longer risks over-relaxing the already-extensible gluten)

Do **not** add starter or salt during autolyse — starter accelerates fermentation (shortcutting the enzyme activity) and salt tightens gluten and slows hydration.

---

## Adding the Starter

After autolyse:
1. Add the starter on top of the autolysed dough
2. Use the pincer method (squeeze through fingers) or fold-and-press until fully incorporated — ~2–3 minutes
3. Rest 20–30 minutes (allows starter to begin distributing its activity before salt goes in)

---

## Adding Salt

Salt is added after the starter is incorporated and has had a short rest.

1. Dissolve salt in the reserved water (this helps distribution)
2. Pour over dough, incorporate with pincer method or folds
3. Salt tightens gluten — the dough will feel noticeably firmer after incorporation

**Salt percentage: 1.8–2%** is the standard range. 2% is common in home baking; professional bakers sometimes use 1.8% to allow slightly faster fermentation. Do not reduce below 1.8% — salt is essential for flavour, crust colour (Maillard reaction), and controlling fermentation rate.

## Bulk Fermentation

The first rise, from when the starter is incorporated until the dough is shaped. This is where most flavour development and gas production occurs.

### What to watch for (not the clock)

The bulk is complete when:
- Dough has increased **40–50% in volume** (for spelt; wheat targets 75–100%)
- Edges where dough meets the container look slightly domed (convex)
- Dough jiggles as a cohesive mass when the container is shaken
- Surface has bubbles and looks alive
- Dough feels lighter and more airy when handled

At 20–22°C with 15% inoculation: expect **3.5–5 hours** for wholemeal spelt. At 24°C+, this can drop to 2.5–3.5 hours. Use visual and tactile cues, not the clock alone.

### Stretch and Folds

Performed during the first half of bulk fermentation to build strength in the dough.

**Standard stretch and fold:**
- Wet your hand, grab one side of the dough, stretch it up until resistance is felt, fold over to the opposite side
- Rotate bowl 90° and repeat — four folds = one set
- Cover and rest 30 minutes between sets

**For wholemeal spelt: 3 sets**, then leave the dough undisturbed. The fragile gluten does not benefit from extra sets.

**Coil fold** (gentler, preferred for fragile doughs):
- Slide hands under the dough, lift it from the centre
- Let each end fold down as you lower it back
- Rotate 90°, repeat once more — that is one coil fold set

**Lamination** (advanced — used for open-crumb white sourdoughs):

Lamination builds structure through thin, layered stretching rather than repeated folding. It is performed once, roughly halfway through bulk fermentation, in place of further stretch and folds.

1. Wet a large work surface generously
2. Pour the dough out and, using wet hands, gently stretch outward in all directions to form a large, thin rectangle — aim for almost translucent in places
3. Fold one third over the centre, then the opposite third on top (letter fold — like folding a letter into thirds)
4. Rotate 90°, letter-fold again
5. Return the dough to its container

If the dough tears before it is fully stretched, stop and rest it for 5 minutes before continuing. After lamination, the dough is delicate — continue with coil folds only, not stretch-and-folds, which are too aggressive at this stage.

After the final set, leave the dough completely undisturbed until bulk is complete.

## Pre-Shape and Bench Rest

1. Turn the dough onto an unfloured surface
2. Using a bench scraper and your hand, drag the dough towards you to build surface tension — do this 2–3 times, gently
3. Cover (inverted bowl or damp cloth) and bench rest for **20–30 minutes**

The bench rest relaxes the gluten enough for final shaping. For spelt, keep this brief — you don't want the dough to relax so much it loses the tension you just built.

---

## Final Shape

For a round (boule):
1. Flip the dough, stretch gently into a rough rectangle
2. Fold the top third down, bottom third up (letter fold), then roll towards you while dragging to build tension
3. Place seam-side up in a well-floured banneton (rice flour is best — it doesn't absorb into the dough)

For spelt: shape **firmly but quickly**. Every additional handling step degrades the gluten. One confident shape is better than repeatedly adjusting.

---

## Cold Retard (Overnight Proof)

Place the shaped loaf (in its banneton, covered) in the fridge at 4–5°C.

**Duration: 8–16 hours** is the typical range. The cold retard:
- Slows fermentation dramatically, giving a long, slow final proof
- Develops flavour (acetic acid production increases at cold temperatures)
- Firms the dough for easier scoring
- Allows you to bake at a convenient time

For spelt, a cold retard is strongly recommended — the cold firms the dough and compensates for the weaker gluten structure, giving better oven spring.

Bake directly from the fridge — do not warm the dough before baking.

## Baking

### Equipment

A **lidded cast iron vessel** (Dutch oven) is the most reliable home method. It traps steam from the dough in the first phase of baking, which:
- Keeps the crust extensible so the loaf can expand fully
- Develops a thin, crackly crust rather than a thick, tough one
- Promotes better oven spring

Preheat the Dutch oven in the oven at **250°C (230°C fan)** for at least 45 minutes before baking.

### Process

1. Score the cold loaf just before baking (a single ear score at ~30–45° angle, or a simple cross)
2. Lower into the hot Dutch oven, replace lid immediately
3. **Bake with lid on: 20 minutes** — steam phase, maximum oven spring
4. **Remove lid, reduce to 220°C (200°C fan): 20–25 minutes** — crust development and browning
5. Loaf is done when it sounds hollow when tapped on the base, and an instant-read thermometer reads **96–98°C** internal temperature

### Cooling

Cool on a wire rack for **at least 1 hour** before cutting. The crumb is still setting during this time — cutting too early gives a gummy interior.

## Diagnosing Common Problems

| Problem | Likely cause |
|---|---|
| Flat loaf, no oven spring | Over-fermented bulk; starter past peak; under-scored |
| Dense, gummy crumb | Under-fermented; cut while still hot |
| Very sour flavour | Over-fermented; starter past peak when used; too high inoculation |
| Collapsed dough, impossible to shape | Spelt over-fermented; too long bulk or too warm |
| Thick, tough crust | Insufficient steam; baked without lid |
| Pale crust | Oven not hot enough; insufficient Maillard reaction |
