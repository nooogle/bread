    Pain de Campagne¶

    A rustic French country loaf built on a rye sourdough starter. The flour blend is mostly white with a measure of wholemeal wheat and a small addition of whole rye, giving the crumb a creamy warmth and subtle earthiness. The rye starter brings a malty, mineral depth and a more complex tang than a white-flour starter can produce — this is a noticeably more interesting loaf to eat than a straight white sourdough, without being challenging to make.

    This bread is more forgiving than the white sourdough — 78% hydration makes handling easier, and the rye starter's vigour gives reliable, active fermentation. The tradeoff is a shorter bulk window; watch the dough, not the clock.

    A Note on Rye Starter Hydration¶

    If you have been maintaining your rye starter at 200% hydration, convert it to 100% before using it for bread. A 200% rye starter is counterproductively liquid: rye's pentosans (complex polysaccharides) absorb water so aggressively that at 200% the starter becomes near-pourable, losing all gas-retention ability, shortening the already-fast peak window, and making it impossible to read when the starter is ready. At 100% hydration — equal weights flour and water — a rye starter is a thick paste, which is correct.

    Converting from 200% to 100%: Do 2–3 feeds using equal weights of rye flour and water. After two feeds the culture is effectively at 100% hydration regardless of starting state. It will be noticeably thicker than you are used to.

    Ongoing maintenance: Feed with equal weights whole rye flour and water. A rye starter at 100% is vigorous and robust — it tolerates a wider temperature range than a wheat starter, recovers quickly after fridge dormancy, and gives a readable peak: a domed top, bubbles throughout, and slight pulling away from the jar sides.

    Formula¶

    All quantities in baker's percentages relative to total flour weight.

    Ingredient % Weight (650g flour)
    Strong white bread flour 85% 553g
    Wholemeal wheat flour 10% 65g
    Whole rye flour 5% 32g
    Water 78% 507g
    Active rye starter (100% hydration) 15% 100g
    Fine sea salt 2% 13g

    Total dough weight: ~1,273g
    Expected baked weight: ~1,080g (roughly 15% baking loss)

    Water split: 487g into autolyse, 20g reserved for dissolving salt.

    Why 15% inoculation? Rye starters are significantly more active than white-flour starters. At 20% inoculation, a rye-starter dough can over-ferment before shaping in a typical kitchen. 15% gives a reliable bulk window of 4–5.5 hours.

    Scaling¶

    To change the loaf size, pick your target flour weight and multiply: - Small loaf (450g flour): multiply all weights by 0.69× - Large loaf (900g flour): multiply all weights by 1.38×

    Equipment¶

    • Digital scales (0.1g or 1g precision)
    • Large mixing bowl or lidded container for bulk ferment
    • Round or oval banneton, ~1kg capacity
    • Dutch oven (cast iron, at least 4L)
    • Bench scraper
    • Lame or razor blade for scoring
    • Instant-read thermometer
    • Large, smooth work surface for lamination (wet, not floured)

    Water Temperature¶

    Target dough temperature after mixing: 24°C.

    Water temp = (Target dough temp × 3) − room temp − flour temp − starter temp

    In a typical 20°C kitchen: 

    Water temp = (24 × 3) − 20 − 20 − 20 = 12°C  →  slightly cool tap water

    Starter Preparation¶

    Reviving from dormancy¶

    Revive with 2–3 feeds at room temperature before bake day. Feed at 100% hydration throughout: equal weights whole rye flour and water.

    Dormancy Revival feeds
    Up to 1 week 1–2 feeds
    1–3 weeks 2–3 feeds
    More than 3 weeks 3 feeds minimum; discard deeper if it smells sharp or chemical

    Each feed: discard to 20g, add 20g rye flour + 20g water. Leave 8–12 hours between feeds at room temperature.

    Build feed¶

    Once reliably active (doubling within 6–8 hours), do one larger feed to produce the 100g needed:

    • Discard to 20g
    • Add 50g rye flour + 50g water
    • Total produced: ~120g — use 100g in the dough, keep 20g as ongoing culture

    Leave 5–8 hours at room temperature until peaked.

    Signs of readiness¶

    • Doubled (or nearly doubled) from fed weight — rye rises less dramatically than wheat; 1.5–2× is normal
    • Top is domed, not flat or sunken
    • Interior full of bubbles throughout; smells tangy, yeasty, and slightly malty
    • Pulls away from the sides of the jar
    • Float test: a small spoonful dropped in water floats

    Schedule¶

    This assumes a starter at peak. The schedule targets an overnight cold proof and baking the following morning.

    Step Time (approx)
    Autolyse T + 0:00
    Add starter T + 1:00
    Add salt T + 1:30
    Stretch and fold set 1 T + 2:00
    Stretch and fold set 2 T + 2:30
    Lamination T + 3:00
    Coil fold 1 T + 3:30
    Coil fold 2 T + 4:00
    End of bulk ferment T + 4:30 – 5:30
    Pre-shape + bench rest When bulk is complete
    Final shape ~30 min after pre-shape
    Into fridge ~5 min after final shape
    Bake next morning T + 14:00 – 20:00

    The bulk window is shorter than for white sourdough. Target 50% volume rise, not 75%. A rye starter runs fast; pushing further produces a slack, over-acidic dough that will spread rather than rise.

    Method¶

    1. Autolyse (T + 0:00)¶

    • Combine 553g strong white flour, 65g wholemeal wheat flour, and 32g whole rye flour in a large bowl; mix the dry flours together first
    • Add 487g water and mix by hand until no dry flour remains — 1–2 minutes
    • Cover and rest 45–60 minutes at room temperature

    2. Add Starter (T + 1:00)¶

    • Pour 100g peaked rye starter over the autolysed dough
    • Incorporate using the pincer method: squeeze through the dough with your fingers, alternating with folding, until no streaks remain — ~2–3 minutes
    • Cover and rest 30 minutes

    The dough will feel extensible but less wet than an 80% hydration white dough — the rye flour adds absorbency. This is correct.

    3. Add Salt (T + 1:30)¶

    • Dissolve 13g fine sea salt in the reserved 20g water
    • Pour over the dough and incorporate with the pincer method until fully mixed — ~1–2 minutes
    • Cover and rest 30 minutes

    4. Stretch and Folds — Sets 1 and 2 (T + 2:00 and T + 2:30)¶

    Perform 2 sets of stretch and folds, 30 minutes apart.

    One set: 1. Wet your hand 2. Grab the far side of the dough, stretch up until resistance, fold to the near side 3. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat — four folds total = one set 4. Cover and rest 30 minutes

    5. Lamination (T + 3:00)¶

    1. Wet the work surface generously — the dough should slide freely
    2. Pour the dough out onto the wet surface
    3. Stretch outward in all directions until a large, thin rectangle — aim for almost translucent in the thinnest areas
    4. If it tears before fully stretched, rest 5 minutes and continue
    5. Fold one third over the centre (letter fold), then fold the opposite third on top
    6. Rotate 90° and letter-fold again
    7. Return to the container; cover and rest 30 minutes

    6. Coil Folds (T + 3:30 onwards)¶

    Perform 2 sets of coil folds, 30 minutes apart.

    One coil fold: 1. Slide both hands under the centre of the dough and lift gently 2. Let one end fall and fold under, then lower and let the other end fold under 3. Rotate 90° and repeat once

    After the second set, leave the dough completely undisturbed for the remainder of bulk fermentation.

    7. Bulk Fermentation¶

    The dough is ready to shape when:

    • Volume has increased by 50%
    • The surface looks gently domed and bubbly, with visible gas just beneath
    • The dough moves as a cohesive, airy mass when the container is jiggled
    • A finger poke springs back slowly, but not all the way

    At 20–22°C, this typically occurs 4–5.5 hours after the starter was added (T + 4:30 to T + 5:30). Warmer kitchens shorten this significantly — a rye starter in a 24°C kitchen can complete bulk in 3.5 hours.

    Do not push to 75% rise. With a rye starter, 50% is the target. Over-fermentation produces a dough too slack and acidic to hold structure in the oven.

    8. Pre-Shape¶

    1. Gently scrape the dough onto a lightly floured surface
    2. Using a bench scraper in one hand and your other hand on top, drag the dough toward you to build surface tension
    3. Repeat 2–3 times, rotating slightly each time, until the dough forms a taut round
    4. Cover with an inverted bowl or damp cloth and bench rest 25–30 minutes

    9. Final Shape¶

    Both boule (round) and bâtard (oval) work well for pain de campagne.

    Boule (classic look): 1. Flour a round banneton generously with rice flour 2. Flip the dough — smooth side down, rough side up 3. Draw the edges up into the centre, then flip and rotate with cupped hands to build surface tension 4. Place seam-side up in the banneton; pinch the seam gently to seal

    Bâtard (same method as white sourdough): 1. Flour an oval banneton generously with rice flour 2. Flip the dough; stretch gently into a rough rectangle 3. Fold the left and right sides in to the centre 4. Roll from the top toward you, dragging back slightly to build tension as you roll 5. Place seam-side up in the banneton

    Cover with a shower cap or cling film.

    10. Cold Retard¶

    • Place the covered banneton in the fridge at 4–5°C
    • Leave 10–16 hours — overnight is ideal; bake straight from the fridge without warming

    The cold retard is particularly important with a rye starter. The slow, cool fermentation develops earthy, complex depth that makes this loaf genuinely interesting — it is the biggest single driver of flavour. A longer retard (14–16 hours) gives a more pronounced character; 10 hours will be cleaner and milder.

    11. Baking¶

    Preheat: Dutch oven (with lid) in the oven at 250°C (230°C fan / 480°F) for at least 45 minutes.

    Score: Invert the banneton onto a piece of baking parchment. Score immediately:

    • Single ear cut: one deep cut at 30–45° along the length of a bâtard — dramatic, gives a strong ear
    • Cross cut: two cuts crossing in the centre of a boule — the classic pain de campagne look
    • Wheat stalk: a long central cut with short diagonal cuts branching off each side — rustic and distinctive

    Cut swiftly and decisively.

    Into the oven: 1. Lift the dough using the parchment, lower carefully into the hot Dutch oven 2. Replace the lid immediately

    Phase Temperature Time
    Lid on (steam) 250°C / 230°C fan 20 min
    Lid off (crust) 220°C / 200°C fan 25–30 min

    The rye content promotes slightly faster Maillard browning than a straight white loaf — a darker bake is correct here. The loaf is done when: - Sounds hollow when tapped on the base - Internal temperature: 96–98°C - Deep golden-brown to dark crust

    Cool on a wire rack for at least 1.5 hours before slicing.

    What to Expect¶

    • Crumb: Semi-open, irregular holes; creamy to slightly warm-toned colour from the rye and wholemeal. Less dramatically open than pure white sourdough, but far from dense
    • Crust: Thick and crackly, often developing a fine craze pattern as it cools
    • Flavour: Earthy, malty depth from the rye starter, with a well-rounded tang and nutty sweetness from the wholemeal — more complex than white sourdough; flavour peaks on day 2 and holds well through day 3
    • Shelf life: 4–5 days well wrapped; the rye content improves moisture retention compared to a straight white loaf

    Variations¶

    Variation Change
    More rye character Increase rye in dough to 10–15%; reduce white flour accordingly; expect a slightly denser, more flavourful crumb
    More open crumb Remove rye from dough entirely (90% white, 10% wholemeal); handle as white sourdough
    More tang Extend cold retard to 16–18 hours; or build a stiff rye levain (60% hydration) 12 hrs before mixing
    Milder, sweeter Reduce cold retard to 10 hours; catch the starter just before peak
    Caraway Add 1–2% caraway seeds (7–13g) with the salt — pairs beautifully with the rye flavour
    Seeded crust Roll the shaped loaf in mixed seeds (sesame, sunflower, nigella) before placing seam-side up in the banneton
