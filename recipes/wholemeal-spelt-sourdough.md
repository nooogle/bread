# Wholemeal Spelt Sourdough

A 100% wholemeal spelt sourdough loaf using traditional techniques. Dense and flavourful with a crackling crust — this is what spelt bread is meant to taste like. Do not expect the open crumb of a high-gluten wheat loaf; spelt's nature is a tighter, more even crumb.

---

## Formula

All quantities in baker's percentages relative to flour weight.

| Ingredient | % | Weight (450g flour) |
|---|---|---|
| Wholemeal spelt flour | 100% | 450g |
| Water (total) | 73% | 329g |
| Active sourdough starter (100% hydration) | 15% | 68g |
| Fine sea salt | 2% | 9g |

**Total dough weight:** ~856g  
**Expected baked weight:** ~720g (roughly 15% baking loss)

### Scaling
To change the loaf size, pick your flour weight and multiply:
- Small loaf (350g flour): multiply all weights by 0.78×
- Large loaf (600g flour): multiply all weights by 1.33×

## Equipment

- Digital scales (0.1g or 1g precision)
- Large mixing bowl or lidded container for bulk ferment
- Banneton (proofing basket), ~700g capacity — or a bowl lined with a well-floured cloth
- Dutch oven (cast iron, at least 4L)
- Bench scraper
- Lame or razor blade for scoring
- Instant-read thermometer

---

## Water Temperature

Target dough temperature after mixing: **24°C**.

Use the following formula to calculate water temperature:
```
Water temp = (Target dough temp × 3) − room temp − flour temp − starter temp
```
In a typical 20°C kitchen with flour at 20°C and starter at 20°C:
```
Water temp = (24 × 3) − 20 − 20 − 20 = 72 − 60 = 12°C  → slightly cool tap water
```
Adjust for your conditions. This matters more in hot weather.

## Starter Revival & Preparation

If your starter has been dormant in the fridge, revive it before baking day.

### Revival feeds

Each feed: discard to 25g, add 25g water + 25g flour (1:1:1 by weight). Leave 10–12 hours at room temperature (20–22°C) between feeds.

| Dormancy | Number of revival feeds |
|---|---|
| Up to 2 weeks | 2 feeds |
| More than 2 weeks | 3 feeds |

A sluggish starter can be encouraged by adding a pinch of wholegrain rye flour to one feed — the extra minerals spur fermentation.

### Build feed

Once the starter reliably doubles within 4–6 hours of a feed, do one final larger feed to produce the 68g needed for the recipe:

- Discard to **30g**
- Add **30g water + 30g flour**
- Total produced: ~90g — use 68g in the dough, keep 22g as ongoing starter

Leave 4–8 hours at room temperature until peaked.

### Signs of readiness

- Doubled from its fed weight
- Top is domed, not flat or sunken
- Interior is bubbly and webby when stirred
- Smells tangy and yeasty — not sharp, alcoholic, or like nail varnish
- Float test: a small spoonful dropped in water floats

## Schedule

This recipe assumes you are starting with a **starter at peak** (just doubled in size). The schedule below targets a same-day bulk fermentation and overnight cold proof for baking the next morning.

| Step | Time (approx) |
|---|---|
| Autolyse | T + 0:00 |
| Add starter | T + 0:45 |
| Add salt | T + 1:15 |
| Stretch and fold set 1 | T + 1:30 |
| Stretch and fold set 2 | T + 2:00 |
| Stretch and fold set 3 | T + 2:30 |
| End of bulk ferment | T + 4:30 – 5:30 |
| Pre-shape + bench rest | T + 4:30 |
| Final shape | T + 5:00 |
| Into fridge (cold retard) | T + 5:10 |
| Bake next morning | T + 16:00 – 20:00 |

All times are approximations. **Watch the dough, not the clock.**

## Method

### 1. Autolyse (T + 0:00)

- Weigh **450g wholemeal spelt flour** into a large bowl
- Weigh **314g water** (hold back 15g for dissolving salt later)
- Mix by hand until no dry flour remains — take ~1–2 minutes, no more
- Cover and rest at room temperature for **30–45 minutes**

The dough will feel rough and shaggy at first. After autolyse it will be smoother, slightly sticky, and extensible.

> **Why hold back water?** The reserved 15g is used to dissolve the salt and add it in a controlled way. It also allows you to adjust hydration if the dough feels too wet or dry after autolyse.

---

### 2. Add Starter (T + 0:45)

- Pour **68g of peaked active starter** over the autolysed dough
- Incorporate using the pincer method: squeeze the starter through the dough with your fingers, alternating with folding — repeat until no streaks remain (~2–3 minutes)
- Cover and rest **30 minutes**

The dough should feel fairly extensible. If it tears easily or feels very fragile, handle even more gently from this point.

---

### 3. Add Salt (T + 1:15)

- Dissolve **9g salt** in the reserved 15g water
- Pour over the dough
- Incorporate with the pincer method until fully mixed — ~1 minute
- The dough will feel noticeably firmer and more cohesive after salt is added
- Cover and rest **15 minutes**

---

### 4. Stretch and Folds (T + 1:30 to T + 2:30)

Perform **3 sets of stretch and folds**, spaced 30 minutes apart.

**One set:**
1. Wet your hand to prevent sticking
2. Grab the dough on the far side, stretch up until resistance, fold over to the near side
3. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat — four folds in total = one set
4. Cover and rest 30 minutes

**For spelt, be gentle.** These folds build structure — but aggressive handling will destroy it. If the dough feels fragile or tears, do fewer folds per set, or switch to coil folds.

**Coil fold alternative** (gentler):
1. Slide both hands under the centre of the dough, lift gently
2. Let one end fall and fold under, then lower and let the other end fold under
3. Rotate 90° and repeat once — one coil fold complete

After the **3rd set**, leave the dough completely undisturbed.

---

### 5. Bulk Fermentation (until ready to shape)

The dough will continue fermenting after the last fold. It is ready to shape when:

- **Volume has increased by ~40–50%** (spelt: not more)
- The surface looks bubbly and alive
- The edges of the dough against the container are slightly domed
- When jiggled, the dough moves as a cohesive, airy mass
- The dough feels lighter and more pillowy than at the start

At 20°C, this typically occurs **3.5–5 hours** after the starter was added. In a warmer kitchen (23–24°C), expect **2.5–3.5 hours**.

> **Spelt warning:** Do not chase a 75–100% rise as you would with wheat. Spelt's weaker gluten means the dough will become unshapeable if taken that far. A 40–50% rise is correct and intentional.

---

### 6. Pre-Shape

1. Turn the dough onto a clean, **unfloured** surface (slight stickiness helps build tension)
2. Using a bench scraper in one hand and your other hand on top, drag the dough towards you in a smooth motion to build surface tension
3. Repeat 2–3 times, rotating slightly each time, until the dough forms a taut round
4. Cover with an inverted bowl or damp cloth and bench rest for **20–25 minutes**

---

### 7. Final Shape

1. Flour your banneton generously with **rice flour** (it doesn't absorb and prevents sticking far better than wheat flour)
2. Flip the dough — smooth side down, rough side up
3. Gently stretch into a rough square/rectangle
4. Fold the top third down, then the bottom third up (letter fold)
5. Roll from the top towards you, using your hands to drag back slightly and build tension as you roll
6. Place **seam-side up** in the banneton
7. Pinch the seam gently to seal, dust the top (which will become the base) with rice flour
8. Cover with a shower cap, cling film, or damp cloth

Shape **firmly and quickly** — one pass is better than fiddling. Every extra touch costs structural integrity.

---

### 8. Cold Retard

- Place the covered banneton in the fridge at **4–5°C**
- Leave for **8–16 hours** — overnight is ideal
- Do not let it warm up before baking; bake straight from the fridge

The cold firms the dough, making it easier to score cleanly and giving better oven spring. Acetic acid production increases at cold temperatures, contributing a pleasant mild tang.

---

### 9. Baking

**Preheat:** Place the Dutch oven (with lid on) in the oven and preheat to **250°C (230°C fan / 480°F)** for at least **45 minutes**. The thermal mass must be fully saturated.

**Score:** Remove the dough from the fridge. Cut a piece of baking parchment to fit the base of your Dutch oven. Invert the banneton onto the parchment — the smooth side is now up. Score immediately using a lame or sharp razor:
- A single curved ear score at ~30–45° to the surface is classic
- Or a simple cross if you prefer a more rustic look
- Cut confidently and quickly — hesitation produces a dragging score, not a clean one

**Into the oven:**
1. Lift the dough using the parchment, lower carefully into the hot Dutch oven
2. Replace the lid immediately
3. **Bake at 250°C with lid on: 20 minutes** — steam phase
4. Remove lid, reduce to **220°C (200°C fan)**
5. **Bake without lid: 20–25 minutes** — crust development

The loaf is done when:
- Deep golden to dark brown crust
- Sounds hollow when tapped on the base
- Internal temperature: **96–98°C**

**Cool on a wire rack for a minimum of 1 hour** before slicing. The crumb continues to set as it cools. Cutting early gives a gummy, undercooked texture even when the bread is fully baked.

---

## What to Expect

- **Crumb:** Close, even, and moist — this is correct for 100% wholemeal spelt. Not the open, holey crumb of white wheat sourdough.
- **Crust:** Thin, crackly, deeply coloured
- **Flavour:** Nutty, slightly sweet, mild tang — spelt's natural sweetness balances the sourdough acidity
- **Shelf life:** Excellent. Keeps well wrapped for 4–5 days; flavour improves on day 2

---

## Variations

| Variation | Change |
|---|---|
| More open crumb | Replace 30% wholemeal spelt with white spelt or strong white wheat |
| More sour flavour | Extend cold retard to 18–20 hours, or increase starter to 20% |
| Faster same-day bake | Increase starter to 20%, skip cold retard, proof at room temp ~2 hours after shaping |
| Seeds | Add 10–15% toasted seeds (sunflower, sesame, caraway) at salt incorporation stage |
| Seeded crust | Roll the shaped loaf in seeds before placing in banneton |
